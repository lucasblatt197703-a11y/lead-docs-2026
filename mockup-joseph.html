01 — Origin

When a Storm
Becomes a Canvas

In 2018, Hurricane Michael tore through the Florida Panhandle — one of the most destructive storms to ever make landfall in the United States. It left behind splintered homes, collapsed beams, and thousands of feet of lumber with nowhere to go.

Joseph Huynh saw something different. He saw a canvas.

Born in Vietnam and self-taught as an artist, Joseph salvages the wreckage of those homes — pine floors, bead board, wall studs — and transforms them into original paintings. Sea turtles, pelicans, coastal wildlife. Rendered in oil on wood that has survived a category 5 hurricane.

"The wood already has a story. I just add one more layer to it."

02 — Material

The Wood Has
Already Survived.

The reclaimed lumber Joseph works with isn't just raw material. It's pine flooring pulled from homes that stood for 150 years. Bead board that absorbed the salt air of the Gulf for generations. Heart pine studs that watched families grow.

Hurricane Michael destroyed those homes in hours. Joseph salvages that wood and gives it new purpose — turning loss into something people want on their walls.

What Makes It Rare

No two pieces of reclaimed wood share the same grain, weathering, or history. Every painting is singular — the texture, the knots, the age rings are part of the composition. You're not buying a print. You're buying a piece of Florida history with art on top of it.

03 — The Artist

Joseph
Huynh

Born in Vietnam, Joseph Huynh came to Florida without formal art training and built his practice entirely through observation, experimentation, and an instinct for the natural world. His primary subjects — sea turtles, pelicans, coastal birds — are rendered with a warmth that feels personal rather than illustrative.

What distinguishes Huynh's work is the material beneath the paint. Every piece is executed on wood salvaged from homes destroyed by Hurricane Michael, the 2018 Category 5 storm that devastated the Florida Panhandle. The wood arrives with its own patina — nail holes, saw marks, knots, grain — and Huynh incorporates all of it into the final composition.

"Chris" — a sea turtle rendered on a plank of 150-year-old reclaimed pine flooring — became one of his most-requested pieces. Collectors specifically ask for prints of it in handmade reclaimed-wood frames.
